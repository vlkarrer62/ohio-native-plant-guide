About This Plant

The Thorned Tree of the Floodplain

Honey locust is a fast-growing, large deciduous tree native to the central hardwood forests of eastern North America, including much of Ohio. In the wild it favours river bottoms, floodplains, and disturbed forest edges — places with full sun and variable moisture — and it is one of the most resilient native trees in the landscape, tolerating drought, compacted soil, salt, and heat with equal indifference.

Two features define it immediately. The first is the thorns — not the modest single spines of a hawthorn but branching clusters of long, sharp thorns on the branches and stems, sometimes reaching eight inches or more. On some wild individuals these extend to the trunk as well, but branch thorns are the more consistent expression; thorn presence and placement varies considerably between trees. The second is the seed pods — long, flat, leathery, and gently twisted, turning from green to deep mahogany brown in autumn, and containing seeds embedded in a sweet, edible pulp that gives the tree its name.

The canopy is airy and fine-textured — the compound leaves cast filtered dappled shade rather than dense shadow — making honey locust one of the few large native trees under which a lawn or understory planting can actually thrive.

Seasonal Interest

A Year in the Life

Spring
Delicate compound leaves emerge late — one of the last native trees to leaf out — opening the canopy to spring wildflowers below.
Early sum.
Small greenish-white flower clusters appear, unassuming but intensely fragrant and heavily visited by bees. Long pods begin forming.
Summer
Fine-textured canopy casts dappled shade. Pods lengthen and flatten through midsummer, turning from green to bronze-brown.
Fall
Long mahogany pods drop and persist; deer, squirrels, and other mammals seek them for the sweet pulp. Foliage turns soft yellow.
Winter
Architectural branching structure revealed; thorns most visible; remaining pods may persist into winter.

Wildlife Support

Who Uses This Tree

Honey locust is less celebrated than oaks for caterpillar support, but its ecological contributions are real and varied — concentrated especially in its flowers and pods rather than its foliage.

The flowers are small and easy to overlook, but intensely fragrant — a sweet, heavy scent that draws pollinators from a distance and makes a blooming honey locust one of the most active bee trees of early summer.

Growing Conditions

Honey locust is among the most adaptable native trees in the Ohio landscape, thriving in conditions that would challenge most species. Its main limitation in designed landscapes is the thorns — on wild forms, formidable enough to warrant careful siting.

Did You Know?

The Stories Behind This Plant

01

The Thorns May Have Evolved to Deter Megafauna That No Longer Exist

The thorns of wild honey locust are remarkable — branching clusters of hardened spines that can exceed eight inches, found most consistently on branches and smaller stems. On some trees they extend to the trunk as well, while on others the trunk is largely smooth; thorn expression varies considerably between individual trees. All forms are far more formidable than anything needed to deter the browsers alive in North America today, and for decades ecologists puzzled over why such an extreme defence would be maintained.

The leading hypothesis points to Pleistocene megafauna — mammoths, mastodons, giant ground sloths, and other large browsers that went extinct roughly 10,000 years ago. These were animals large enough to strip bark and consume wood; the honey locust's thorns may have evolved to deter them. The pods, too, fit this picture — large, sweet, and nutritionally suited to big animals that would carry seeds far. The tree is, in a real sense, still adapted to a world that no longer exists.

02

The Sweet Pods Are Edible — and Were Used

The pulp inside a ripe honey locust pod is genuinely sweet — reportedly tasting faintly of honey or molasses, which accounts for both the common name and the species name triacanthos being paired with it since early botanical records. Indigenous communities and early European settlers used the pods as a food source, fermenting the pulp into drinks or using it as a sweetener. The seeds are nutritionally rich and have been used as forage for livestock. The sweetness is not incidental — it is the evolutionary incentive that encouraged large animals to eat and disperse the seeds.

03

It Fixes Nitrogen Like a Garden Legume

Honey locust is a member of the legume family (Fabaceae), which means it forms a symbiotic relationship with soil bacteria that convert atmospheric nitrogen into a form plants can use — the same process that makes clover and beans such useful additions to a garden. A mature honey locust enriches the soil around its roots over time, making it a quietly beneficial presence in mixed plantings. This nitrogen-fixing ability also contributes to its remarkable tolerance of poor, lean soils where other trees struggle.

04

The Thornless Tree in Your Neighbourhood Is Probably Not Wild

Most honey locusts planted along streets and in parks are thornless cultivars — Gleditsia triacanthos var. inermis, or named selections bred specifically for their absence of spines and sometimes reduced pod production. These trees carry the same fine-textured canopy and adaptable constitution as the wild form, but lack the thorns and often produce few or no pods. They are useful landscape trees, but stripped of the two most ecologically interesting features — the megafauna-scale defence and the wildlife food source — that make the wild tree so botanically remarkable.
