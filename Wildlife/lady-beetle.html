About This Beetle

The Garden's Favorite Predator

Lady beetles — the ladybugs and ladybirds of nursery rhymes — are small, domed beetles, and among the most useful predators a garden has. Both the glossy adults and their spiny, alligator-like larvae hunt aphids, scale insects, and mites, and a single adult can work through dozens of aphids in a day. They follow their prey: wherever aphids gather, lady beetles tend to arrive.

There's a twist worth knowing in Ohio. The ladybug most people now see — the one that swarms onto warm house walls each fall — is often the introduced multicolored Asian lady beetle, brought over for pest control. Meanwhile several native species, including the nine-spotted that was once common enough to be named New York's state insect, have grown scarce. The causes aren't fully settled, but competition with the introduced beetles — which will even eat native eggs and larvae — is a leading suspect.

The same garden habits that feed aphids to the food web help lady beetles most: tolerate a few colonies and you feed both the adults and their hungry larvae — and give the struggling native species a foothold that bought beetles and sprays never will.

What Brings Them In

Prey & Plants

Lady beetles aren't tied to any one plant — they go where the food is. That means two things draw them into a garden: colonies of soft-bodied prey to hunt, and flowers offering pollen and nectar to tide them over when prey is thin. The first stop is almost always the aphids. Tap any plant to read its profile.

Life Cycle

A Year in the Life

Spring
Adults leave their winter shelter, feed, mate, and lay clusters of small yellow-orange eggs on leaves close to aphid colonies.
Late spring
Eggs hatch into spiny, alligator-like larvae that roam the plants devouring aphids — eating even more, for their size, than the adults.
Summer
Each larva fastens to a leaf and pupates, then emerges as a familiar domed adult. There may be more than one generation in a season.
Fall
Adults feed heavily, then look for sheltered places to wait out winter — leaf litter, logs, bark, and rock crevices (or, for the Asian species, the walls of houses).
Winter
Adults gather in sheltered spots, often many together, and pass the cold dormant until spring warmth wakes them.

How to Support It

Garden Actions That Help

Supporting lady beetles is mostly a matter of staying out of their way — and remembering that the natives need it most.

Tolerate aphids. They're the main food for adults and larvae alike. A few aphid colonies keep lady beetles fed and breeding right where you can see them.

Skip the insecticides. Sprays kill lady beetles outright — and their larvae, which most people don't recognize and so don't think to protect.

Leave winter shelter. Undisturbed leaf litter, fallen logs, and standing stems give adults somewhere to ride out the cold. A yard scoured bare in fall has nowhere for them to go.

Learn the larva. The spiny, blue-grey, orange-flecked larva looks nothing like the adult and is often mistaken for a pest. Knowing it on sight is one of the most useful things a gardener can learn.

Skip the bag of "released" ladybugs. Those are usually wild-collected rather than raised, tend to simply fly away, and can carry disease into local populations. Feeding the wild beetles you already have works better.

Did You Know?

The Stories Behind This Beetle

01

Not a Bug at All

Despite the name, a ladybug is not a true bug — it's a beetle, which is why entomologists prefer "lady beetle" or "ladybird beetle." The affectionate name is usually traced to medieval Europe, where the beetles were dedicated to "Our Lady": the red cloak and the spots were read as symbols in old religious imagery, and the little aphid-eaters that saved the crops were seen as her gift.

02

The Alligator Stage

A lady beetle's larva looks nothing like the tidy domed adult: it's elongated, spiny, and slate-blue with orange flecks, prowling leaves like a tiny alligator. It is, if anything, an even hungrier aphid-eater than the adult — and because it's unrecognizable, it's too often squashed as a pest. When it's done growing, it glues itself to a leaf, pupates, and emerges as the beetle everyone knows.

03

A Native in Trouble

The nine-spotted lady beetle was once so common that New York named it the state insect — and then it nearly vanished, along with other natives like the two-spotted. Introduced lady beetles spread as the natives declined, and while the exact causes remain debated, competition and the newcomers preying on native eggs and young are leading suspects.

You can help map what's left: the Lost Ladybug Project, a long-running citizen-science effort, asks people to photograph the lady beetles they find and report which species are still out there.

04

Bitter Blood & Bright Warnings

The bold red-and-black is no accident — it's a warning that the beetle tastes terrible. When threatened, a lady beetle does reflex bleeding, oozing a drop of bitter yellow fluid from its leg joints to repel whatever's bothering it. The introduced Asian species does this readily, which is part of why it stains and smells when it finds its way indoors for the winter.

Related Species

Ohio Lady Beetles

Ohio has many lady beetles, native and introduced. Telling them apart often comes down to the pronotum — the shield behind the head — as much as the spots.

Native · Most Common

Convergent Lady Beetle

Hippodamia convergens

The most common native, orange-red with up to a dozen black spots and two telltale white lines converging on the black pronotum. Common still, though it too has slipped as the introduced species spread.

Pink-Spotted Lady Beetle

Coleomegilla maculata

An elongated native, pink to rosy-red with black spots, unusual for eating a great deal of pollen alongside its aphids. Common in gardens, meadows, and crop fields.

Nine-Spotted Lady Beetle

Coccinella novemnotata

Once abundant enough to be named New York's state insect, the "C-9" has become rare across its old range and is the emblem of native lady beetle decline. A genuine find if you spot one.

Asian Lady Beetle

Harmonia axyridis

Hugely variable, from yellow to deep red with anywhere from no spots to many, marked by a black "M" on the white pronotum. Introduced for pest control, it's the one that gathers on warm walls each fall and can nip.
