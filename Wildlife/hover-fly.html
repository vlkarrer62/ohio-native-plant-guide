About This Fly

The Bee That Isn't

Hover flies — also called flower flies — are the great impostors of the garden. Many wear the same yellow-and-black bands as bees and wasps, hover around the same flowers, and fool most predators (and most people). But they are flies, with no sting whatsoever. Once you know the tells, they are easy to call: a hover fly has one pair of wings rather than two, very short stubby antennae, oversized eyes, and the habit of hanging dead still in midair before darting away.

They earn their keep twice over. The adults are among the most important pollinators after bees, working flowers all season; and the larvae of many species are voracious predators of aphids and other soft-bodied pests. A single garden hover fly is, in effect, a pollinator and a pest-control service in one small body.

Few garden insects offer as much as a hover fly: the adults deliver pollination and natural pest control in one animal — feeding flowers as they nectar, while their aphid-eating young quietly keep infestations in check. Supporting them means tolerating a few aphids and skipping the spray.

What It Visits in This Garden

Hover flies have short mouthparts, so they favour open, shallow flowers where nectar and pollen are easy to reach — umbels and flat composites especially. As adults they are steady pollinators across the season; just remember that the next generation needs aphids to eat, so the most hover-fly-friendly garden is one that doesn't try to be spotless. Life Cycle

A Year in the Life

Spring
Adults appear at the first flowers; females seek out aphid colonies and lay eggs among them, close to a ready food supply for the young.
Late spring
Eggs hatch into legless larvae that hunt aphids on stems and leaves — a single larva can clear hundreds before it pupates.
Summer
Several overlapping generations. Adults hover and feed at open flowers, pollinating as they go, and numbers build through the warm months.
Fall
Late adults nectar at the last blooms; some species migrate south in large numbers, while others prepare to overwinter in place.
Winter
Depending on the species, hover flies pass winter as larvae or pupae in soil and leaf litter, or as sheltered adults — varied across the family.

How to Support It

Garden Actions That Help

Hover flies repay a garden twice — as pollinators and as pest control — and they ask for very little in return.

Plant open, shallow flowers. Their short mouthparts do best at umbels like Golden Alexanders and flat composites like asters, goldenrod, and wingstem. Aim for something in bloom from spring through fall.

Tolerate aphids. This is the hard one for tidy gardeners: hover fly larvae eat aphids, so a few aphids are food, not failure. Hose them off or spray them and you remove the predators' meals — and usually the predators with them.

Skip the insecticides. Broad-spectrum sprays kill the larvae directly and wipe out the soft-bodied prey they depend on.

Leave a few damp, untidy corners. Some larvae develop in moist litter, leaf debris, and rot holes; a perfectly scoured garden offers them nowhere to grow up.

Did You Know?

The Stories Behind This Fly

01

A Fly in a Bee's Clothing

Hover flies are classic Batesian mimics: harmless animals that copy the warning colours of dangerous ones. By wearing the yellow-and-black bands of bees and wasps, a stingless fly borrows their protection, and many predators leave it alone just in case.

The disguise is good, but not perfect. A hover fly has only one pair of wings where bees and wasps have two; its antennae are short and stubby rather than long; its eyes are large and fly-like; and it hovers in a way few bees bother to. Once you have those tells, the costume stops fooling you.

02

Aphid Patrol

The larvae of many hover flies are among the most useful predators in a garden. Blind, legless, and slug-like, a single larva can eat hundreds of aphids as it grows, reaching into colonies and clearing them plant by plant. Females often lay their eggs right in the middle of an aphid outbreak, so the young hatch surrounded by food — which is exactly why a few aphids are worth leaving alone.

03

Holding Still in Midair

The family is named for the hover: the ability to hang almost perfectly motionless in the air, then dart to a new spot and freeze again. It takes very fast, finely controlled wingbeats, helped by a feature unique to the family — a false vein, the vena spuria, that stiffens the wing. Males often hover to hold a patch of air and court passing females.

04

Rat-Tailed Maggots & Other Lives

Not every hover fly larva hunts aphids. The larva of the common drone fly lives in stagnant, oxygen-poor water and breathes through a long, telescoping tail — the so-called "rat-tailed maggot," its tail working like a snorkel reaching to the surface. Across the family the larvae are remarkably varied: predators, filter-feeders, scavengers, and a few that live inside the nests of ants and bees.

Related Species

Ohio Hover Flies

Ohio has dozens of hover flies, from tiny aphid-hunters to big, convincing wasp mimics. A few of the most often seen show the range of the family.

Most Familiar

Common Drone Fly

Eristalis tenax

A stout, convincing honeybee mimic, abundant on flowers into fall. Its larva is the "rat-tailed maggot" of stagnant water — the classic introduction to just how unlike its disguise a hover fly can be.
