About This Bee

The Long Antennae of Late Summer

Long-horned bees are robust, fast-flying, densely furred bees, usually tawny brown with pale bands of hair across the abdomen. The name belongs to the males: their antennae are extraordinarily long, in some species nearly as long as the body itself, sweeping back over the bee as it works a flower. The females are more soberly built, with antennae of ordinary length and a thick brush of hairs on the hind legs for carrying pollen.

As a group — the tribe Eucerini — they are closely tied to the composites, the daisy family that carries late summer and fall. Many of the females are pollen specialists, gathering mainly from sunflowers, coneflowers, and their kin, and their flight is timed to that bloom. Most nest in bare ground, sometimes in large aggregations of many burrows in one sunny patch; the males don't tend the nest, but patrol the flowers by day and gather to roost on stems at dusk.

When the prairie composites open in late summer, these are among their dedicated bees — which is why a garden of native sunflowers and coneflowers, with a little bare ground nearby, is a long-horned bee garden whether you planned it or not.

What They Visit

Built for Composites

More than most bees, long-horned bees follow the daisy family. Their summer-to-fall flight is timed to the composites, and many of the females gather pollen almost entirely from them. A garden rich in native sunflowers, coneflowers, and their kin — with bloom carried late — is what these bees are looking for. They overlap on these flowers with the bumble bee and sweat bee. Life Cycle

A Year in the Life

Early–mid summer
Adults emerge from their underground nests, timed to the opening of summer composites. The long-antennaed males appear first, patrolling flowers in search of females.
Mid–late summer
Foraging peaks. Females stock their ground nests one cell at a time, packing each with composite pollen before laying an egg and sealing it.
Late summer
Nesting winds down; both sexes feed at the last flowers, and the males gather to roost in clusters on stems through the cool nights.
Fall
The last adults fade as the composites finish. The season's brood remains underground, sealed in its cells as larvae.
Winter
The next generation overwinters underground in its brood cells, emerging the following summer to meet the bloom again.

How to Support It

Garden Actions That Help

A long-horned bee garden is, more or less, a composite garden with some open ground left in it.

Plant native composites. Sunflowers, coneflowers, black-eyed susans and their kin are exactly what these bees are built for — and the more of them, the better.

Keep the late season blooming. Long-horned bees peak when many gardens are winding down. Goldenrods, asters, and late sunflowers carry them through.

Leave bare, sunny ground. Ground-nesters need it, and an aggregation can return to the same patch year after year if it's left alone.

Skip the insecticides. Sprays kill foraging bees and contaminate the pollen carried home to the brood.

Let a nest patch be. Many small holes in a patch of bare, sunny soil are a good sign, not a problem — leave them undisturbed through the season.

Did You Know?

The Stories Behind This Bee

01

Antennae You Can't Miss

The "long-horned" name is all about the males. Their antennae are remarkably long — in some species nearly the length of the whole bee — and once you've noticed them, they're the easiest way to know what you're looking at. The females have antennae of ordinary length, so a long-horned silhouette on a sunflower is almost always a male out patrolling for a mate.

02

Built for Sunflowers

Many long-horned bees are specialists: their females gather pollen mainly from the daisy family, and their whole year is keyed to its bloom, emerging as the summer composites open and finishing as the last asters fade. It's a tight partnership — and it means that losing the native composites means losing the bee. Plant the flowers and the bees tend to follow.

03

Sleeping in Bunches

Male long-horned bees don't return to a nest at night. Instead, at dusk they settle onto stems and dry flower heads to sleep — and in some species several will gather together in a roosting cluster, gripping the stem with their jaws and legs. Coming upon a little knot of sleeping bees on a seed head at dawn is one of the quieter pleasures of a late-summer garden.

04

The Squash-Bee Cousins

The squash bees are close relatives in the same tribe, and specialists of a different kind — tied to the flowers of squash, pumpkins, and gourds. They fly at dawn when the blossoms open, and are often found napping inside a closed squash flower later in the day. If you grow squash, you've likely hosted a long-horned bee's cousin without knowing it.

Ohio's Long-Horned Bees

Eucerini is a tribe of robust, often composite-loving bees, told in the field most easily by the males' long antennae. A few you may meet around an Ohio garden — and one familiar cousin.
