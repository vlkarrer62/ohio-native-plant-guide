About This Bee

Big, Fuzzy, and Social

Bumble bees are the large, round, densely furred bees of the garden — most of them banded in black and gold, a few carrying a band of orange or rust. Their thick pile and their habit of warming their own flight muscles let them work in cool, overcast weather that keeps smaller bees grounded, so they're often the first bees out in spring and the last in fall. A long tongue lets them reach into deep, tubular flowers that many bees can't.

They are also among the best buzz pollinators there are. Gripping a flower, a bumble bee can vibrate its flight muscles to shake pollen loose — a trick called sonication that some flowers depend on entirely. And unlike a solitary bee, a bumble bee lives in an annual colony: a single queen, overwintered alone, founds a nest each spring in a cavity — often an abandoned rodent burrow or a tussock of grass — and raises a season's worth of workers before the cycle begins again.

Because they fly cold and buzz-pollinate, bumble bees do work at the edges of the season and the edges of what other bees can do — which is why a garden that blooms early, blooms late, and leaves rough corners for nesting matters so much to them.

Flowers Through the Season

Bumble bees forage widely, but their long tongues and buzz make them especially good at deep tube flowers and at blooms that hold their pollen tightly. What matters most is an unbroken season: early flowers for the founding queens in spring, and late flowers for next year's queens before winter. Life Cycle

A Colony in a Year

Early spring
A lone queen, overwintered underground, emerges and feeds, then searches for a nest cavity — often an abandoned rodent burrow, a grass tussock, or a sheltered hollow.
Late spring
She founds the colony alone, building wax cells and raising the first brood of workers herself on stored pollen and nectar.
Summer
Workers take over the foraging and the colony grows; this is the busy season, with bees coming and going from the nest all day.
Late summer–fall
The colony rears new queens and males, which leave to mate. The old queen, the workers, and the males then die off as the season ends.
Winter
Only the newly mated queens survive, each waiting out the cold alone, dormant underground, until spring calls her out to begin again.

How to Support It

Garden Actions That Help

Bumble bees reward a garden that thinks about the whole season and tolerates a little wildness.

Bloom early and bloom late. Early flowers feed the founding queens when little else is open; late flowers fuel next year's queens before winter. The shoulders of the season matter as much as its peak.

Plant deep and buzz-pollinated natives. Tube flowers and pollen-rich blooms reward a long tongue and a good buzz — and put bumble bees to work where they do their best.

Leave rough corners for nests. Tussocky grass, an untidy bank, an old burrow left alone — these are where queens found their colonies. A yard mown and cleared to the edges offers nowhere to nest.

Skip the insecticides. Sprays kill foraging bees and can contaminate the pollen carried home to the colony.

Mow and dig thoughtfully. A ground nest can be destroyed in a single mid-season mowing or excavation. Where you can, leave some ground undisturbed from spring through fall.

Did You Know?

The Stories Behind This Bee

01

The Buzz in Buzz Pollination

Some flowers hold their pollen inside tubes that only open under vibration. A bumble bee grips the flower, decouples its wings, and vibrates its flight muscles against it — a high buzz that shakes the pollen out in a cloud. Called sonication, it's something honey bees can't really do, and it's why bumble bees are so valued as pollinators of tomatoes, peppers, and a range of native blooms.

02

Warm-Blooded, Almost

A bumble bee can shiver — vibrating its flight muscles without moving its wings — to warm itself before flight. Together with its thick fur, this lets it fly on cool, grey mornings when most bees stay put, and is why bumble bees are so often the first bees of spring and the last to give up in fall.

03

A Colony That Lasts One Summer

Unlike a honey bee hive, which lives on for years, a bumble bee colony lasts a single season. It begins with one queen doing every job herself — building, foraging, brooding — until her first workers hatch and take over. By fall the colony has produced the next generation of queens and then dies, leaving only those young, mated queens to carry the line through winter.

04

The Ones in Trouble

Not all bumble bees are doing well. Several once-common species have declined in recent decades, and the rusty-patched bumble bee — once widespread across this region — is now federally listed as endangered and only rarely recorded in Ohio. The reasons are debated and likely multiple, but a flower-rich, pesticide-free garden with undisturbed places to nest is part of what helps the bumble bees that remain.

Related Species

Ohio's Bumble Bees

Bombus is a genus of social, fuzzy bees told apart largely by their colour bands. A few of the ones you may meet — and one you most likely won't — around an Ohio garden.

Most Common

Common Eastern Bumble Bee

Bombus impatiens

By far the most common bumble bee in the region and a fixture of gardens: a golden-yellow thorax over a mostly black abdomen with one yellow band. Adaptable, abundant, and the one most people picture.

Brown-belted Bumble Bee
