About This Insect

The Herd at the Bottom of the Food Web

Aphids are small, soft-bodied insects that feed by tapping into a plant's phloem and drinking its sap, usually crowding onto tender stems and new growth. On a false sunflower you'll often find the red ones — likely a native Uroleucon — lined up by the dozens along a stem. In numbers they can stress a plant and shrink its blooms, which is why they have a reputation as pests.

But step back and an aphid colony looks less like a problem and more like a pasture. Aphids are the base of an entire predator web: ladybugs, lacewings, hover fly larvae, parasitoid wasps, soldier beetles, and birds feeding nestlings all depend on them. They also excrete sugary honeydew that feeds ants, bees, and wasps. Few insects are eaten by more things — which makes the humble aphid one of the busiest feeding stations in the garden.

In a native or meadow planting, a tolerated aphid colony is rarely a crisis — it is food for dozens of beneficial insects. Leave it be and the ladybugs, lacewings, and hover fly larvae usually arrive to feed; reach for the spray and you remove their meal along with them.

Plant Relationships

Where You'll Find Them

Most aphids are fussy eaters — a given species sticks to one plant or one closely related group, which is exactly why your red aphids appear on the false sunflower and nowhere else in the bed. The large reddish composite aphids (Uroleucon) work the sunflower family especially, so the same kind of cluster can turn up across these native composites. Tap any plant to read its profile.

Life Cycle

A Year in the Life

Spring
Eggs that overwintered on the host plant hatch into wingless females — the "stem mothers" — which settle on fresh new growth and begin feeding.
Late spring
Without mating, those females bear live young that are clones of themselves, generation after generation. Colonies build fast along stems and buds.
Summer
When a colony grows crowded or the plant tires, winged females appear and fly off to start new colonies elsewhere — often on the same kind of plant.
Fall
As days shorten, a generation of males and egg-laying females is produced. After mating, the females lay the eggs that will carry the line through winter.
Winter
In our climate most species pass the cold as eggs tucked on or near the host plant; the adults die back. Details vary widely from species to species.

Living With Them

How to Handle an Aphid Colony

Aphids sit at an odd crossing point — a sap-feeder that can stress a plant, and at the same time the most-eaten insect in the garden. In a native planting the balance usually tips toward leaving them be.

Tolerate a few colonies. In a meadow or wildflower setting they rarely spoil the display, and every colony feeds beneficial insects. The hover fly in particular lays its eggs right among them.

Skip the insecticides. Sprays kill the ladybugs, lacewings, and hover fly larvae that keep aphids down — which often makes the next outbreak worse, not better.

Let the predators catch up. Given a week or two, the natural enemies usually find a colony on their own; a garden with diverse flowers and untidy edges keeps them nearby.

If you must, go gentle. A sharp spray of water, or running a finger along the stem, knocks numbers back without chemicals and without harming everything else.

Did You Know?

The Stories Behind This Insect

01

Born Already Pregnant

For most of the growing season aphid colonies are entirely female. They reproduce without mating, giving live birth to clones of themselves — and the trick goes a step further: a newborn aphid already carries the developing embryos of the next generation inside her, nested like a set of matryoshka dolls.

That telescoping of generations is why a colony seems to appear from nowhere and double in days. Only in fall does a generation of males and egg-laying females arrive to produce the eggs that overwinter.

02

The Twin Tailpipes

The two little tubes at an aphid's rear end — the cornicles — are the surest way to know you're looking at an aphid; almost nothing else has them. They are not for honeydew, as is often assumed. When the aphid is seized, the cornicles release a quick-setting waxy droplet and an alarm scent that warns the rest of the colony to drop off the plant and scatter.

03

Ant Cows

Aphids excrete honeydew, a sugary fluid left over from all that sap. Ants prize it — so much that many species tend aphid colonies like livestock, stroking them to harvest the honeydew and driving off ladybugs and other predators in return. Some ants go further still, carrying aphids to fresh growth and even sheltering their eggs through the winter.

04

Food for the Whole Garden

Almost everything eats aphids. Ladybugs and their alligator-like larvae, lacewing larvae, hover fly larvae, soldier beetles, and birds gathering food for nestlings all rely on them — and tiny parasitoid wasps lay eggs inside them, leaving behind papery brown "mummies." A tolerated aphid colony, in other words, is a feeding station that pulls beneficial insects into the garden and keeps them there.

Related Species

Aphids You'll Meet in Ohio

Ohio has hundreds of aphid species, and telling them apart often takes a microscope. A few, though, are distinctive enough to know on sight — starting with the one on your false sunflower.

On Your False Sunflower

Red Composite Aphid

Uroleucon sp.

Large, reddish, native aphids that specialize on the sunflower family — sunflowers, goldenrods, asters, and false sunflower. The red ones lined up along your Heliopsis stems are most likely this genus; the exact species is hard to pin down without a microscope.

Largest in North America

Giant Bark Aphid

Longistigma caryae

A native giant — the largest aphid on the continent — with very long legs that make it look almost like a tiny spider. It clusters on the bark and twigs of oaks, maples, and hickories rather than on garden flowers.

NativeLargestOn bark

Bright Yellow

Oleander Aphid

Aphis nerii

An introduced aphid, vivid yellow-orange with black legs, that masses on milkweeds and dogbane. Conspicuous and easy to recognize, though not native to the region.

The Woolly Ones

Woolly Aphids

Subfamily Eriosomatinae

A group coated in white, waxy fluff that drifts from the body like lint. Found on the stems, twigs, and roots of trees and shrubs, they're sometimes mistaken for tiny flying tufts of cotton.
