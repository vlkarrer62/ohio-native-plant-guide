About This Insect

Lace Wings, Lion Larvae

Green lacewings are slight, pale-green insects with long antennae and two pairs of large, clear wings webbed with a fine network of veins — the lacework that gives them their name. They drift through the garden at dusk and often come to porch lights, and many are marked by metallic, coppery-gold eyes that seem to glow when they catch the light. For all their delicacy, the family is one of the most useful a garden has.

The hunting, though, is mostly the work of the young. A lacewing larva is a flattened, spindle-shaped creature with a pair of curved, hollow jaws, and it is a relentless predator of aphids and other soft-bodied prey — so much so that it has long been called the aphid lion. The adults of many species are gentler, feeding on nectar, pollen, and the honeydew that aphids leave behind, though in some it is the adults that hunt too.

It's the unglamorous larva that does the work: a single aphid lion can clear large numbers of aphids before it pupates — which is why a few tolerated aphid colonies, rather than a sprayed and spotless garden, are what keep lacewings breeding where you need them.

What Brings Them In

Prey & Plants

Like most generalist predators, lacewings follow the food rather than any single plant. Two things draw them into a garden: colonies of soft-bodied prey for the larvae to hunt, and open flowers offering the pollen, nectar, and honeydew that carry many adults along. The first stop is almost always the aphids — the same colonies that feed the lady beetle and hover fly. Tap any plant to read its profile.

Life Cycle

A Year in the Life

Late spring
Adults become active in the warm dusk, feed, and mate; females lay tiny eggs, each set atop a slender silk stalk, on leaves near aphid colonies.
Summer
Eggs hatch into spindle-shaped aphid-lion larvae that prowl the foliage, seizing aphids and other soft-bodied prey with their curved jaws. There may be several generations.
Summer–fall
A full-grown larva spins a small round silk cocoon, pupates, and emerges as a new lacy adult to repeat the cycle.
Fall
Late adults feed on. In some species (such as the common Chrysoperla) the adults seek shelter to overwinter, and may shift to a brownish or pinkish hue as the cold sets in.
Winter
The cold is passed dormant — as a prepupa within its cocoon, or as a sheltering adult tucked into litter, bark, or outbuildings — depending on the species.

How to Support It

Garden Actions That Help

Supporting lacewings, like supporting most predators, is largely a matter of leaving the food web intact and getting out of the way.

Tolerate aphids. They're the main food for the larvae. A few aphid colonies left in place keep aphid lions fed and lacewings breeding right where you can see them.

Skip the insecticides. Sprays kill lacewings outright — and their larvae, which few people recognize and so don't think to spare.

Offer flowers for the adults. Pollen- and nectar-rich native plants carry the adults of many species between bouts of egg-laying, and draw them into the garden in the first place.

Ease off the night lighting. Lacewings fly at dusk and into the night, and bright outdoor lights pull them off course. Darker, shielded lighting helps them — and the fireflies sharing the same summer nights.

Learn the larva and the eggs. The spindle-shaped, sickle-jawed aphid lion looks nothing like the delicate adult, and the eggs on their fine stalks are unmistakable once you know them. Recognizing both keeps you from clearing away a helper by mistake.

Did You Know?

The Stories Behind This Insect

01

The Aphid Lion

The larva earns the name. Its curved jaws are hollow: it seizes an aphid, injects digestive fluids, and draws the liquefied meal back out through the same jaws, leaving an empty husk. Some species go a step further and pile the drained skins and other debris onto their own backs — a kind of walking camouflage that helps them pass unnoticed among their prey.

02

Eggs on Stilts

A female lacewing often lays each egg on the tip of a fine, hair-like silk stalk standing up from the leaf. It's one of the most distinctive sights in the insect world — and not just decoration. The stalks tend to hold the eggs apart from one another and out of easy reach, so that the first hungry larva to hatch is less likely to simply turn and eat its unhatched siblings. Once you've seen them, they're an easy way to know lacewings are at work.

03

Golden Eyes at the Porch Light

Many green lacewings carry brilliant metallic eyes, coppery to gold, which is how the genus Chrysopa — "golden eye" — got its name. The adults are creatures of dusk and darkness, and like many night-fliers they're drawn to lights, which is where most people first meet one: a pale-green, long-winged visitor turning slow circles around the porch lamp on a summer evening.

04

A Gentle Adult — Mostly

For all the ferocity of the larva, the adults of many species are mild, sipping nectar, pollen, and honeydew rather than hunting — though in some lacewings the adults prey as well. Handled, a few will give off a faint, musky odor, which has earned them the nickname "stink flies." The familiar garden lacewings are also reared and sold commercially as a living, larva-powered form of aphid control.

Related Species

Ohio's Lacewings & Kin

Lacewings belong to the order Neuroptera — the "net-winged" insects — a small but distinctive group whose larvae are nearly all predators. A few of its members you may meet in and around an Ohio garden.

Most Common

Common Green Lacewing

Chrysoperla spp.

The familiar pale-green, golden-eyed lacewing of gardens and porch lights. Its adults feed largely on nectar, pollen, and honeydew, while the aphid-lion larvae do the hunting. This is the group most often reared and sold for pest control.

Goldeneye Lacewing

Chrysopa oculata

A common native green lacewing with especially vivid golden eyes. Unlike the gentler Chrysoperla, its adults are predatory too, and it's among the lacewings most likely to give off a defensive musky odor when handled.

Related Family

Brown Lacewing

Family Hemerobiidae

A smaller, browner cousin, less noticed than the green lacewings but just as useful — both larvae and adults prey on aphids and other small insects. Often active in cooler stretches of the season when green lacewings are scarce.

Distant Cousin

Antlion

Family Myrmeleontidae

Another net-winged relative, with delicate, damselfly-like adults. The larvae of many species — the "doodlebugs" — dig small conical pits in dry, sheltered soil and lie in wait at the bottom for ants and other prey to tumble in.
