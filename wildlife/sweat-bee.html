About This Bee

Small Bees, Brilliant and Busy

Sweat bees are a large family of mostly small native bees, and the showiest of them are among the most striking insects in the garden: a metallic emerald or brassy green that catches the light like polished metal. The bicolored striped sweat bee pairs a green head and thorax with a black-and-yellow banded abdomen, while others — like the pure green sweat bee — are brilliant green from end to end. Many more are tiny and dark, easy to pass over, but they too are out working the flowers.

As a group they are generalist pollinators, visiting a wide range of blooms and carrying pollen on branched hairs and, in the females, a brush of hairs on the hind legs. Most are ground-nesters, digging slender burrows in bare or sparsely planted soil; a few nest in rotting wood. The family name comes from a habit some have of landing on skin to drink perspiration for its salts — a harmless visit, and the reason a small green bee sometimes alights on a warm arm in summer.

Small native bees like these do an outsized share of the quiet pollination in a garden — and the two things they most need are simple: a long season of varied flowers and some bare ground to nest in.

What They Visit

Flowers Through the Season

Sweat bees are generalists, working whatever is in bloom, but small open and shallow flowers suit their size especially well. A garden that offers a long, unbroken succession of native flowers — from the first spring umbels to the last fall asters — keeps them fed across their whole flight. They forage the same flowers as the carpenter bee and hover fly. Tap any plant to read its profile.

Life Cycle

A Year in the Life

Spring
Overwintered females emerge, dig or reopen slender ground nests, and begin foraging — packing each brood cell with a ball of pollen and nectar before laying an egg on it.
Late spring
Foraging is at its height; the bees work the flowers steadily. In some species a first brood matures and helps tend the nest, since sweat bees range from solitary to social.
Summer
New bees emerge and generations overlap. There may be more than one brood in a season, depending on the species and the weather.
Fall
The last bees of the year mate. The newly mated females — next year's foundresses — feed up for winter, while the males die off.
Winter
In many species the mated females wait out the cold underground, dormant in the soil or a sheltered cavity, until spring warmth brings them out again.

How to Support It

Garden Actions That Help

Sweat bees ask for little: flowers to work and open soil to nest in. A garden built for them is built for most native bees.

Plant a long season of bloom. Aim for something flowering from spring through fall, with plenty of small, open flowers. Variety across the season matters more than any single showy plant.

Leave some bare ground. Many sweat bees nest in bare or thinly planted soil; a yard mulched and turfed wall to wall leaves them nowhere to dig. A few sunny, undisturbed open patches go a long way.

Skip the insecticides. Sprays kill foraging bees directly and can contaminate the pollen they carry back to the nest.

Ease up on tidiness. Undisturbed soil, lighter mulch, and a little untended ground all give ground-nesters a place to live.

Don't swat the one on your arm. A sweat bee landing on skin is only after salt, not a sting. Brush it off gently and let it go; only the females sting, mildly, and only if pressed.

Did You Know?

The Stories Behind This Bee

01

Why "Sweat Bee"

The name comes from a habit some of these bees have of landing on skin to drink perspiration, drawn to its salts and moisture. It's a harmless visit — they're after minerals, not blood, and the sting (only the females have one) is mild and rarely given. The best response to a sweat bee on your arm is simply to brush it gently away.

02

Living Jewels

The metallic green and gold of the brightest sweat bees isn't pigment at all. It's structural colour — produced by microscopic texture in the exoskeleton that scatters light, the same trick behind a hummingbird's throat or a soap bubble's shimmer. It's why a tiny bee, caught in the sun, can flash like a chip of polished metal.

03

Every Kind of Society

Sweat bees are famous among biologists for their social range. Across the family — and sometimes even within a single species, depending on conditions — you'll find bees living entirely alone, bees sharing a nest entrance, and bees with a true division of labour between a queen and her daughters. That flexibility makes them a favourite for studying how social life evolves in the first place.

04

Underfoot, Mostly

Most sweat bees nest in the ground, digging a narrow tunnel to a cluster of brood cells, each stocked with a pollen ball. The only sign at the surface is often a pencil-width hole with a little mound of excavated soil. They're harmless neighbours — and a reason that a patch of bare, sunny ground is worth leaving undisturbed. A few, like the pure green sweat bee, nest instead in rotting wood.

Related Species

Ohio's Sweat Bees

Halictidae is a large and varied family, from dazzling metallic greens to small, dark, easily overlooked bees. A few you may meet around an Ohio garden.

Most Conspicuous

Bicolored Striped Sweat Bee

Agapostemon virescens

The eye-catching one: a brilliant metallic-green head and thorax above a black abdomen banded with pale yellow. Common and conspicuous in gardens and meadows, and a frequent flower visitor through the warm months.

Pure Green Sweat Bee
