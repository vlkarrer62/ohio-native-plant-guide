About This Plant

The Cup That Holds the Rain

Cup plant is one of the tallest perennials of the moist prairie — a coarse, robust species on a distinctly square stem, set with large, rough, opposite leaves. At each node the bases of those paired leaves are fused right around the stem, forming a shallow bowl, or cup, that catches and holds rainwater and dew. It is the feature that names the plant, and you can find small reservoirs of water held up its stem after a storm.

Through mid- and late summer the branched top carries many bright yellow, sunflower-like heads, each with a ring of even rays around a darker disc. It grows in moist prairies, wet meadows, stream banks, and savanna openings, in full sun to part shade, often forming broad colonies.

Cup plant is a true multi-resource species: its long run of yellow heads offers nectar and pollen to bees and butterflies through summer, its large seeds feed goldfinches and other birds into fall, and the rainwater held in its leaf-cups is reported to be used by birds and insects — several jobs from one plant.

Early sum.

Heads set large, flat seeds that goldfinches and other birds work over; the tall, sturdy stems stand without flopping.
Winter
The strong dried stems and seed heads persist well into winter, holding structure and cover before being cut back.

Wildlife Support

Who Visits

Cup plant's long bloom and abundant open heads draw a wide range of insects through summer — bumblebees and other native bees, butterflies, wasps, and flies. As a tall, resource-rich prairie plant it also supports some specialist insects, though those associations vary by region.

Beyond insects, cup plant is valued for birds: goldfinches and other songbirds feed heavily on its ripe seed, and the rainwater held in its leaf-cups is reported to draw birds and insects to drink. The standing stems add cover and structure through winter.

Care & Cultivation

Growing Conditions

Cup plant is easy, long-lived, and emphatically large. Give it sun and average-to-moist, fertile soil and it will reach well above head height on stems sturdy enough that they rarely need staking. The thing to plan for is scale and spread: it forms colonies by rhizome and self-sows, so it belongs at the back of a planting or in a naturalised area with room to grow.

Did You Know?

The Stories Behind This Plant

01

A Cup at Every Node

Where each pair of leaves meets the stem, their bases are fused all the way around it, making a shallow bowl that catches rain and dew. The cups can hold a surprising amount of water well up the plant, and birds and insects are reported to drink from them — though, as with any standing water, the cups can also collect debris and the odd mosquito larva. Either way, it is the feature that gives the plant its name.

02

Square Stems, Resinous Sap

Run a hand up the stem and you can feel it is distinctly four-angled — square in cross-section, and rigid enough that even an eight-foot plant rarely flops. Break one and it exudes a sticky, aromatic resin; that resin gives the genus its other common name, rosinweed, and was historically chewed as a kind of gum. It is mentioned here as background, not as a use.

03

Built for Finches

Cup plant's large, flat seeds ripen in late summer and fall and are a favourite of goldfinches in particular, which will work the drying heads while the stems still stand. Between nectar, pollen, seed, the water in its cups, and winter cover, few single plants in the prairie palette do as many different jobs across the year.

04

Cup Plant & the Silphiums

Cup plant belongs to Silphium, the silphiums or rosinweeds — tall, deep-rooted prairie perennials with yellow heads. What is striking about the genus is how differently each species handles its leaves: compass plant slices them into fern-like blades that tend to align north–south, prairie dock raises enormous paddle-shaped leaves from the ground, and cup plant fuses its into cups. The genus is the subject of the showcase below.

Genus Silphium

Meet the Silphiums

Silphium — the silphiums or rosinweeds — are tall, coarse, deep-rooted prairie perennials with yellow heads and resinous stems. What most sets the species apart is how each one handles its leaves. Cup plant, profiled above, is the moisture-loving member and the most adaptable in gardens; its relatives are creatures of drier prairie.
