About This Plant

Garden Value, Honestly Considered

Catmint is a Mediterranean perennial — a hybrid of European Nepeta species — that has become one of the most widely used edging and border plants in American gardens. It forms a spreading grey-green mound covered in lavender-blue flower spikes from late spring through much of summer, and if cut back after the first flush, it rebounds with a second. The foliage is aromatic, softly textured, and strongly deer-resistant.

It is not native to Ohio and does not host the caterpillars of native moths and butterflies the way Ohio native plants do. But it is genuinely and consistently attractive to a wide range of pollinators — particularly bees — and its exceptionally long bloom window means it is providing that resource from May through September, bridging gaps when some native plantings are between flushes.

A note on native value: Catmint contributes nectar and pollen but offers limited caterpillar hosting — the ecological foundation that native plants provide. In a garden actively building toward native plantings, it functions well as a bridge plant, supporting pollinators while the native community matures around it.

Seasonal Interest

A Long Season of Bloom

Spring
Fresh grey-green mounds emerge early; flower buds appear by mid-spring. One of the earlier garden perennials to come into bloom.
Early sum.
First peak bloom — lavender-blue spikes at their densest, heavily visited by bees from morning through midday on warm days.
Midsummer
Cut back by half after first flush for a tidy rebound; reblooms within 3–4 weeks with fresh foliage and a full second flush of flowers.
Late sum.
Second bloom peak through August and into September — coincides with monarch migration and the final bee foraging push before winter.
Fall
Bloom tapers; foliage remains soft grey-green until frost. Cut back in fall or leave for winter structure — either works.

Did You Know?

Notes on This Plant

01

Why Hover Flies Are Worth Noticing

The hover fly observed on this plant looks like a small bee or wasp — the striped abdomen is a deliberate visual mimicry. But it is a fly, in the family Syrphidae, and its ecological role is double-sided in a way few insects match. As an adult it is a pollinator, visiting flowers for nectar and pollen. As a larva, depending on the species, it is either an aphid predator — one larva can consume hundreds of aphids in its development — or a decomposer working in leaf litter and soil.

Seeing a hover fly on a plant is a small sign that the garden is complex enough to support this relationship. They arrive where there is something to hunt or something to eat, and their presence tells you more about the garden's food web than the plant alone.

02

The Mint Family and Bees

Catmint belongs to the Lamiaceae — the mint family — one of the most bee-friendly plant families in the world. The characteristic two-lipped flower (one broad lower lip, two narrow upper lobes) functions almost as a landing platform designed for bees. The nectar is accessible, the pollen is abundant, and the flower's architecture directs a visiting bee into exactly the right position for pollination. Many of the most valuable garden plants for pollinators — salvias, lavender, monarda, mountain mint — are Lamiaceae members. Catmint's consistent visitor numbers reflect family-level architecture as much as any individual charm.

03

Nepetalactone — The Compound Cats and Insects Both Notice

The aromatic quality of catmint foliage comes from nepetalactone, an iridoid compound found throughout the genus. It is the same compound that produces the well-known response in cats. Less well-known is that nepetalactone also has insect-repellent properties — it is more effective than DEET against certain insects — while simultaneously attracting others. The leaf chemistry that deters deer and repels some insects also signals bees and certain flies that this is a productive foraging plant. The same molecule plays different roles depending on who encounters it.
