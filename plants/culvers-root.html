About This Plant

A Spire of Whorls and Wands

Culver's root grows as a tall, upright stem — often unbranched below the flowers — set with leaves arranged in whorls, several narrow, lance-shaped, finely toothed leaves circling the stem at each node, stacked in tiers up its height. The overall effect, even before bloom, is strongly vertical and tiered.

In mid- to late summer the top of each stem carries its flowering cluster: a tall central spike with several shorter spikes rising from around its base, together forming a candelabra. The spikes are densely packed with small tubular flowers, usually white and occasionally tinged pale pink or lilac, that open gradually from the base of each spike upward. Protruding stamens give the spikes a soft, brushy look. It grows in moist to medium ground — prairies, wet meadows, the edges of open woods, and stream banks — in full sun to part shade.

Each candelabra holds a great many small, accessible flowers at a height that stands above much of the surrounding meadow. Opening over an extended period in mid- to late summer, the spikes offer a steady source of nectar and pollen to a wide range of bees — bumblebees most visibly — during a stretch of the season when their concentrated, easy-to-reach flowers are heavily worked.

Seasonal Interest

A Year in the Life

Spring
Whorled foliage emerges and the stems rise quickly, building height through late spring while remaining clothed in tiers of fresh green leaves.
Early sum.
Stems reach near their full height. The flower spikes form at the top, still green and in bud, the candelabra shape already visible before any flowers open.
Midsummer
Peak bloom — the white spikes open from the base upward, brushy with protruding stamens. Bees, especially bumblebees, work the candelabra heavily through July into August.
Late sum.
Flowering finishes at the spike tips. Spent spikes begin to dry and turn tan; the foliage and the upright form remain standing.
Fall–Winter
The slender seed spikes darken to brown and persist, holding the plant's vertical silhouette and some seed well into winter for structure and wildlife.

Wildlife Support

Who Visits

The densely packed spikes present many small, shallow, tubular flowers at once, accessible to a broad range of visitors. Culver's root is most strongly associated with bees, and is worked particularly hard by bumblebees, but its open flowers also draw flies, wasps, and some butterflies.

Through fall and winter, the standing seed spikes add vertical structure and hold seed above the snow. Culver's root is also reported to be among the plants deer tend not to favour, though browsing pressure varies from place to place and no plant is reliably untouched.

Care & Cultivation

Growing Conditions

Culver's root is easy in the moist, fertile conditions it prefers, and rewards an open, sunny position with strong, upright stems. In dry soil or heavy shade it tends to be shorter, may scorch at the leaf edges, and the tall stems are more likely to lean.

SoilAverage to rich; tolerates clay; does best where the soil holds some moisture

Did You Know?

The Stories Behind This Plant

01

Built Like a Candelabra

The flowering cluster is not a single spike but a group of them. A tall central spike rises from the stem tip, and several shorter spikes lift from around its base, each curving upward — the arrangement that gives the plant its branched, candelabra silhouette. The number of spikes varies from plant to plant and stem to stem.

Within each spike, the small tubular flowers are packed closely and open in sequence from the bottom toward the tip, so a single spike can show open flowers, buds, and finished flowers at the same time. The stamens reach beyond the petals, and en masse they give the white spikes their characteristic soft, brushy texture.

02

A Speedwell by Another Name

The genus name Veronicastrum means, roughly, "resembling Veronica" — the speedwells. The Latin suffix -astrum signals an incomplete or partial likeness, a "wild" or "false" version of the named plant. Culver's root was long classified close to the speedwells and placed in the figwort family; current treatments put it in the plantain family, Plantaginaceae, alongside its Veronica relatives.

03

The "Culver" in Culver's Root

The common name is usually traced to a physician — recorded in older sources as Dr. Culver or Coulvera — associated with the historical medicinal use of the plant's root. The dried root was used in early American and Indigenous medicine, chiefly as a strong purgative. It is worth noting that the fresh root is considered toxic and was prepared only after drying and aging; this is a matter of historical record rather than a home remedy, and the plant is not used this way today.

04

Standing Through Winter

After bloom, the spikes dry in place rather than collapsing, narrowing into slender brown seed spikes that hold the plant's upright shape. Left standing, they keep a strong vertical line in the fall and winter garden, offer some seed to wildlife, and provide overwintering structure — one reason many gardeners delay cutting the stems back until late winter.

Vertical Company

Spires of the Summer Meadow

Culver's root is the only Veronicastrum native to Ohio, so it has no close cousins to compare here. Several other native wildflowers, though, share its tall, spike-flowered habit and its taste for sunny, moist ground — plants that read similarly at a distance and often grow in the same places.
