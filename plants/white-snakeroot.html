About This Plant

Bright White in Deep Shade

White snakeroot grows as a bushy, branched plant on slender, often dark stems, set with opposite leaves that are broadly oval, pointed, and coarsely toothed. For most of the year it reads as quiet green foliage in the shade. Then, from late summer into fall, the stem tips open flat-topped clusters of small white flower heads — each head a tuft of tiny tubular disc florets with no ray petals, so the effect is soft and frothy rather than daisy-like.

It grows in woodland interiors and along shaded edges, in thickets, and on stream banks, in medium to moist humusy soil, though it tolerates dry shade once established. It is one of relatively few native plants that bring bright white bloom to shaded ground late in the season, when most of the woodland's flowering is finished.

Opening late, in shade, when much of the surrounding woodland has finished flowering, white snakeroot offers an accessible source of late-season nectar and pollen to a wide range of small insects. Its broad, flat clusters present many shallow flowers at once, and in many shaded gardens it is among the more active patches for pollinators in the weeks before frost.

Seasonal Interest

A Year in the Life

Spring
Mounds of opposite, toothed leaves emerge on dark stems, filling shaded ground with quiet green foliage.
Summer
Stems lengthen and branch. The flat flower clusters form at the tips, still green and tightly in bud, the bushy shape filling out.
Late sum.
The clusters begin to open — small white florets brightening the shade, frothy with protruding styles, drawing the first late-season visitors.
Fall
Peak bloom. The plant reads as drifts of bright white in shaded ground, worked steadily by small bees, wasps, flies, and butterflies in the weeks before frost.
Winter
Each floret gives way to a tiny seed tipped with a tuft of white hairs; the fluffy seed heads scatter on the wind and the dried stems stand a while.

Wildlife Support

Who Visits

The flat clusters present many tiny, shallow, accessible flowers at once, late in the season, so white snakeroot draws a broad mix of small visitors — short-tongued native bees, wasps, flies, beetles, and small butterflies — through fall. Its foliage is also reported to support the caterpillars of some moths, though the associations vary by region.

Care & Cultivation

Growing Conditions

White snakeroot is undemanding in the shade and part shade it prefers, taking ordinary to rich woodland soil and tolerating dry shade once established — a useful trait, since few flowering plants thrive in that setting. Its main quirk is enthusiasm: it self-sows freely, which makes it excellent for naturalizing a shaded area and something to keep an eye on in a tidy bed.

Did You Know?

The Stories Behind This Plant

01

Flowers Without Petals

Each bright white head is not a single flower but a small cluster of tiny tubular florets, all of the disc type — there are no ray petals at all, the strap-shaped "petals" that ring a daisy. What gives the heads their soft, fuzzy look is the flower parts themselves: the styles protrude well beyond the florets, and en masse they read as a frothy white tuft.

The heads are gathered into broad, flat-topped clusters, and they open over a stretch of weeks, so a plant can carry open flowers and buds together. After bloom each floret yields a single small seed.

02

Milk Sickness

White snakeroot contains a compound called tremetol. When cattle graze the plant, the toxin can pass into their milk and meat — and from there to people. In the early 1800s this caused outbreaks of "milk sickness" across the Ohio Valley and the frontier Midwest, an often-fatal illness that emptied settlements before its cause was understood.

The death of Nancy Hanks Lincoln — Abraham Lincoln's mother — in 1818 is widely attributed to milk sickness. The plant's role was not formally established until decades later; by tradition, a frontier physician, Anna Pierce Hobbs Bixby, is credited with tracing the illness to the plant. It is recounted here as history and as a caution: the plant is toxic, and this is not guidance for any use.

03

The "Snakeroot" Name

The common name comes from a historical folk use of the root as a purported treatment for snakebite — a use reflected in the names of several unrelated "snakeroots." That history is the source of the name only; all parts of the plant are toxic, and this is offered as background rather than a recommendation.

04

Seeds on the Wind

Like many in the aster family, each finished floret produces a small seed tipped with a tuft of fine white hairs — a tiny parachute that carries it on the wind, the way a dandelion's seeds travel. This is how white snakeroot self-sows so readily and colonizes shaded ground.

The dark-stemmed, purple-leaved plant sold in nurseries as 'Chocolate' is a selected garden form of this same native species, grown for its foliage colour against the white flowers.

White Flowers of the Fall Woods

White snakeroot is one of several pale-flowered plants that bloom in shaded and edge ground late in the year, and a couple of them are easy to confuse with it. The quickest tells are the flower shape — fuzzy tufts versus daisy rays — and where the plant is growing.

White Wood Aster

Eurybia divaricata

A low, wiry shade aster that blooms the same season in similar ground, but its flowers are small white daisies with distinct ray petals around a yellow centre, and its lower leaves are heart-shaped. The ray petals are the easiest way to tell it from snakeroot's fuzzy tufts.

Boneset

Eupatorium perfoliatum

A relative with similar flat-topped clusters of fuzzy white flowers, but told apart by its leaves, which are joined around the stem so the stem appears to pass through them, and by its home ground: wet meadows and marsh edges in full sun, not dry woodland shade.

Zigzag Goldenrod

Solidago flexicaulis

A goldenrod of the same woodland shade, named for its bending, zigzag stem, with small yellow flower clusters in the leaf axils in fall. Not a look-alike but a frequent neighbour, its yellow setting off snakeroot's white in the same shaded ground.
