About This Plant

The Leaf the Stem Grows Through

Boneset is a stout, upright perennial of wet ground, easiest to recognize by its leaves. They are long, lance-shaped, wrinkled, and finely toothed, and each opposite pair is joined at the base around the stem — so the stem appears to pass straight through a single, pierced leaf. The botanical name records exactly this: perfoliatum, "through the leaf."

From mid- or late summer into fall, the branched stem tips carry broad, flat-topped clusters of small, soft white flower heads, fuzzy with protruding flower parts and held above the foliage. It grows in wet meadows, marsh and pond edges, low fields, ditches, and stream banks — in full sun to part shade, on ground that stays moist to wet.

Blooming late and presenting many small, shallow, easily reached flowers on broad flat heads, boneset is among the more heavily visited late-season nectar plants of the wet meadow. Its open clusters draw a wide cross-section of insects — bees, wasps, flies, beetles, and butterflies — in the weeks when the meadow's flowering is winding down.

A Year in the Life

Spring
Stems rise from the crown, the perfoliate leaf pairs clasping the stem as they expand — the pierced-leaf shape clear from early on.
Early sum.
The plant gains its full height on moist ground and branches near the top, where the flat flower clusters form in bud, still green.
Late sum.
Peak bloom — broad flat heads of soft white flowers open above the foliage and are worked steadily by a wide range of late-season insects.
Fall
Flowering continues, then fades to tan as the heads set seed. The upright stems and wrinkled leaves hold their shape.
Winter
Dried seed heads and stems stand through much of winter, offering structure and some seed before being cut back in late winter.

Wildlife Support

Who Visits

The broad, flat heads present many tiny, shallow flowers at a height that is easy to reach, late in the season, so boneset draws an unusually wide range of visitors — short- and long-tongued bees, many wasps and flies, beetles, and butterflies. Its foliage is also reported to support the caterpillars of several moths, though those associations vary by place.

Care & Cultivation

Growing Conditions

Boneset is easy where it has the consistent moisture it wants, and rewards an open, sunny position with sturdy, upright stems and full flat heads. It tolerates clay and periodic flooding, and even seasonal standing water; what it does not take well is drought, where it tends to wilt, brown at the edges, and stay short.

Planting Partners

Grows Well With

Boneset belongs to wet, sunny ground — rain gardens, wet meadows, and the edges of ponds and streams. Its broad white heads sit well among the taller verticals and the warm late colours of the wet meadow, and it pairs naturally with other moisture-loving natives.

Did You Know?

The Stories Behind This Plant

01

Pierced by Its Own Stem

Boneset's signature is its perfoliate leaves: each opposite pair is fully joined at the base, forming what looks like one long leaf with the stem running straight through its centre. The species name, perfoliatum, means just that — "through the leaf."

The joined leaves form a shallow cup at each node. It has been suggested that this catches rain and dew, and may make the stem harder for crawling insects to climb, though how much it matters to the plant is not settled. Whatever its function, it is the quickest way to tell boneset from its many white-flowered relatives.

02

The Boneset Name

The common name comes from the plant's long history in folk and early medicine, where a bitter tea of the dried leaves was taken for fevers, colds, and influenza. It was particularly associated with "breakbone fever" — an old name for dengue and similar illnesses, named for the deep, aching pain in the bones — and the plant's reputation for easing that ache is one traditional source of the name "boneset."

A second tradition ties the name to the doctrine of signatures: the joined, "bone-knitting" look of the perfoliate leaves was read as a sign the plant could help mend bones. Boneset contains pyrrolizidine alkaloids and is considered toxic; this history is recounted as background and caution, not as guidance for any use.

03

A Crowd in Late Summer

Boneset is noted for the sheer breadth of insects its flowers draw. Because the heads are flat, open, and shallow, their nectar is within reach of short-tongued visitors that deeper flowers exclude — small bees, many wasps and flies, and beetles — alongside the longer-tongued bees and butterflies. Arriving late in the season, it adds an accessible nectar source just as much of the surrounding bloom is finishing.

04

One of Many Thoroughworts

Boneset was once placed in a very large genus, Eupatorium, the thoroughworts. Botanists have since divided much of that group: the Joe-Pye weeds moved to Eutrochium, white snakeroot to Ageratina, and the mistflowers to Conoclinium. Boneset itself kept the old name and remains Eupatorium perfoliatum — so its many flat-topped, fuzzy-flowered cousins are relatives at a slightly greater distance than their shared look suggests.

Genus Eupatorium

Meet the Bonesets

"Boneset" and "thoroughwort" are both names for the genus Eupatorium — upright, late-blooming natives that carry flat-topped clusters of small white flowers. Common boneset, profiled above, is the wet-meadow member most often grown; its relatives sort out mainly by leaf shape and the ground they prefer.
